🏋🏼 Heavy tails III: Kesten's theorem

November 2023

Motivation: ARCH models

In 2003, Robert F. Engle won the Nobel prize in economy for his innovative methods in time-series analysis; to put it sharply, Engle introduced ARCH models into economics. In his seminal 1982 paper (30k+ citations), he wanted to model the time-series (yt)(y_t)(yt​) representing the inflation in the UK. Most previous models used a simple autoregressive model of the form yt+1=αyt+εty_{t+1} = \alpha y_t + \varepsilon_tyt+1​=αyt​+εt​, where εt\varepsilon_tεt​ is an external Gaussian noise with variance σ2\sigma^2σ2 and α<1\alpha<1α<1 a "forgetting" parameter. The problem with these models is that the variance of the inflation at time t+1t+1t+1 knowing the inflation at time ttt, that is Var(yt+1∣yt)\mathrm{Var}(y_{t+1}|y_t)Var(yt+1​∣yt​), is simply the variance of Var(εt)=σ2\mathrm{Var}(\varepsilon_t)=\sigma^2Var(εt​)=σ2, which does not depend on yty_tyt​.

Engle wanted a model where the conditional variance would depend on yty_tyt​: there are good reasons to think that volatility is sticky. The model he came up with (equations (1)-(3) in his paper) is simply

yt+1=εt×α+βyt2.\begin{aligned} &y_{t+1} = \varepsilon_t \times \sqrt{\alpha + \beta y_t^2} . \end{aligned}​yt+1​=εt​×α+βyt2​​.​

In other words, the variance of yt+1y_{t+1}yt+1​ given yty_tyt​ is α+βyt2\alpha + \beta y_t^2α+βyt2​. This is the simplest of Auto-Regressive Conditionally Heteroscedastic (ARCH) models. Upon squaring everything in (1), the equation becomes

yt+12=αεt2+βεt2yt2.y_{t+1}^2 = \alpha \varepsilon^2_t + \beta \varepsilon_t^2 y_t^2.yt+12​=αεt2​+βεt2​yt2​.

This is a linear recursion in yt2y_t^2yt2​. In the paper, Engle introduced a few variations and crafted statistical methods to estimate the parameters and their significance.

A central question in this way of modelling things is: how does yny_nyn​ behave in the long term? Does yny_nyn​ stay stable, can it take extremely large values (crises, shocks and crashes), and if so, at which frequency?

If yt2y_t^2yt2​ converges in distribution towards a random variable YYY, then (2) shows that YYY and αε2+βε2Y\alpha \varepsilon^2 + \beta \varepsilon^2 Yαε2+βε2Y must have the same probability distribution, where ε\varepsilonε is an N(0,σ2)\mathscr{N}(0,\sigma^2)N(0,σ2), independent of YYY. This is an instance of a very general kind of equations, called affine distributional equations: they are equations of the form

Y=lawAY+BY \stackrel{\mathrm{law}}{=} AY+BY=lawAY+B

where A,BA,BA,B are random variables independent of YYY. It turns out that these equations are generally impossible to solve. However, a theorem of Harry Kesten states, perhaps not so intuitively, that the law of any solution must have a heavy tail: in contrast with, say, Gaussian distributions, for which taking extremely large values (« shocks ») has an exponentially small probability, heavy-tailed distributions can take large values with polynomially small probability, which is… not so rare at all!

Here is a small simulation over 10610^6106 periods of time and parameters α=0.1,β=1,σ=0.1\alpha=0.1, \beta=1, \sigma = 0.1α=0.1,β=1,σ=0.1. The histogram indicates that y(t)y(t)y(t) seems to have heavy tails, that is, the probability of observing a unusually large value is polynomially small (and not exponentially small).

This is one of Kesten's most famous results, published in 1973 in Acta Mathematica.

Kesten's theorems

From now on, we'll study the solutions of the equation

X=lawAX+B X \stackrel{\mathrm{law}}{=} AX+B X=lawAX+B

where XXX is a random variable over Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd, AAA is a random matrix and BBB a random vector, both independent of XXX. The goal is to present Kesten's theorem, which states that on certain conditions on AAA and BBB, the solution XXX must have heavy tails; that is, P(X>x)\mathbb{P}(X>x)P(X>x) decreases polynomially fast, and not exponentially fast as for Gaussian variables. This result is somehow mind-blowing in its precision, since we have access to the exact tail exponent.

A full, rigorous proof would be packed with technicalities; I'll only show a simili-proof in dimension 111, which, although not being complete, was sufficiently clear and idea-driven to convince me that Kesten's result holds. I'm also including a simili-proof of the Renewal theorem, which might be of independent interest.

One-dimensional case

For simplicity, we restrict to the case where A,BA,BA,B are independant and have a density with respect to the Lebesgue measure. In general, it is not obvious that solutions to (4) exist, but a former result by Kesten states that it is the case if E[ln⁡∣A∣]<∞\mathbb{E}[\ln |A|]<\inftyE[ln∣A∣]<∞.

Kesten's theorem (1973).

(i) Assume that A>0A>0A>0 almost surely and that there is an s>0s>0s>0 such that E[∣A∣s]=1,E[∣A∣s(ln⁡∣A∣)+]<∞,E[∣B∣s]<1.\begin{aligned}&\mathbb{E}[|A|^s]=1, &&\mathbb{E}[|A|^s (\ln |A|)_+]<\infty, &&\mathbb{E}[|B|^s]<1.\end{aligned}​E[∣A∣s]=1,​​E[∣A∣s(ln∣A∣)+​]<∞,​​E[∣B∣s]<1.​ Then, there are two constants c±c_\pmc±​ such that when x→∞x\to \inftyx→∞, P(X>x)∼c+x−s,P(X<−x)∼c−x−s.\begin{aligned}&\mathbb{P}(X>x)\sim c_+ x^{-s},&&&\mathbb{P}(X<-x) \sim c_- x^{-s}.\end{aligned}​P(X>x)∼c+​x−s,​​​P(X<−x)∼c−​x−s.​ (ii) The same result holds if AAA can take positive and negative values, and in this case c+=c−c_+ = c_-c+​=c−​.

Sketch of proof in the 1d case

I'll only sketch the proof ideas in the subcase of (i) where in addition, BBB is nonnegative. In this case, we can safely assume that XXX is nonnegative by conditionning over the set {X>0}\{X>0\}{X>0}. We set f(x)=esxP(X>ex)f(x) = e^{sx}\mathbb{P}(X > e^x)f(x)=esxP(X>ex); our final goal is to prove that f(x)f(x)f(x) converges towards some constant when x→+∞x\to+\inftyx→+∞, the −∞-\infty−∞ case being identical.

The recursion (4) shows that f(x)=esxP(AX+B>ex)f(x) = e^{sx}\mathbb{P}(AX + B > e^x)f(x)=esxP(AX+B>ex). However, if xxx is very large, we could guess that P(AX+B>ex)\mathbb{P}(AX + B > e^x)P(AX+B>ex) is close to P(AX>ex)\mathbb{P}(AX > e^x)P(AX>ex). This is the origin of the first trick of the proof, which is to artificially write

f(x)=esx(P(AX+B>ex)−P(AX>ex))+esxP(AX>ex).\begin{aligned} f(x) &= e^{sx}(\mathbb{P}(AX+B>e^x) - \mathbb{P}(AX>e^x)) + e^{sx}\mathbb{P}(AX>e^x). \end{aligned}f(x)​=esx(P(AX+B>ex)−P(AX>ex))+esxP(AX>ex).​

Let us note ggg the first term on the right:

g(x)=esx(P(AX+B>ex)−P(AX>ex)).g(x)=e^{sx}(\mathbb{P}(AX+B>e^x) - \mathbb{P}(AX>e^x)).g(x)=esx(P(AX+B>ex)−P(AX>ex)).

For the second term, we can express it in terms of fff by using E[As]=1\mathbb{E}[A^s]=1E[As]=1:

esxP(X>ex−ln⁡A)=E[Ases(x−ln⁡(A))1X>ex−ln⁡A].\begin{aligned} e^{sx}\mathbb{P}(X>e^{x - \ln A}) &= \mathbb{E}[A^s e^{s(x-\ln(A))}\mathbf{1}_{X>e^{x - \ln A}}]. \end{aligned}esxP(X>ex−lnA)​=E[Ases(x−ln(A))1X>ex−lnA​].​

Now, here comes the second trick: the change of measure. We assumed that E[As]=1\mathbb{E}[A^s]=1E[As]=1, hence we can define a probability distribution by Es[φ]=E[Asφ]\mathbb{E}_s[\varphi] = \mathbb{E}[A^s \varphi]Es​[φ]=E[Asφ], and the expression above becomes Es[f(x−ln⁡A)]\mathbb{E}_s[f(x- \ln A)]Es​[f(x−lnA)]. Overall, we obtain that fff is a solution of the following equation: 

∀x⩾0,f(x)=g(x)+Es[f(x−ln⁡A)].\forall x \geqslant 0, \qquad f(x) = g(x) + \mathbb{E}_s[f(x - \ln A)].∀x⩾0,f(x)=g(x)+Es​[f(x−lnA)].

If μs\mu_sμs​ is the density of log⁡A\log AlogA under the measure Ps\mathbb{P}_sPs​, this equation becomes f(x)=g(x)+f⋆μ(x)f(x) = g(x) + f\star \mu(x)f(x)=g(x)+f⋆μ(x) where ⋆\star⋆ denotes the convolution operator. Such equations are called convolution equations and they can be studied using classical probability theory. The main result of Renewal Theory, exposed later below, shows that

  • if ggg is « directly Riemann integrable »; 

  • and if Es[ln⁡A]>0\mathbb{E}_s[\ln A]>0Es​[lnA]>0, which is the same thing as E[Asln⁡(A)]>0\mathbb{E}[A^s \ln(A)]>0E[Asln(A)]>0;

then there is only one solution to this equation, and more crucially it satisfies

lim⁡x→∞f(x)=∫0∞g(u)duEs[ln⁡A]=∫0∞g(u)duE[Asln⁡A]=:c.\lim_{x\to \infty}f(x) = \frac{\int_0^\infty g(u)du}{\mathbb{E}_s[\ln A]} = \frac{\int_0^\infty g(u)du}{\mathbb{E}[A^s \ln A]}=:c.x→∞lim​f(x)=Es​[lnA]∫0∞​g(u)du​=E[AslnA]∫0∞​g(u)du​=:c.

If E[Asln⁡A]>0\mathbb{E}[A^s \ln A]>0E[AslnA]>0, as we assumed, then c>0c>0c>0, because ggg is a nonzero positive function so its integral is nonzero. Consequently,

lim⁡x→∞esxP(X>ex)=c. \lim_{x\to \infty}e^{sx}\mathbb{P}(X>e^x) = c.x→∞lim​esxP(X>ex)=c.

This is equivalent to P(X>x)∼cx−s\mathbb{P}(X>x) \sim cx^{-s}P(X>x)∼cx−s, as requested. There is, however, a serious catch: to apply the main Renewal theorem we need to check the two conditions listed above. The second one is nothing but an assumption. However, the first one needs ggg to be "directly Riemann integrable", which is indeed very difficult to check. I won't do this part at all[1].

Multi-dimensional case

In the multi-dimensional case, a similar theorem holds. One can find in the litterature a host of different settings regarding the random matrix AAA and the random vector BBB; for simplicity I'll stick to the case where AAA is positive definite almost surely, BBB has all entries nonnegative almost surely, and A,BA,BA,B have densities with respect to the Lebesgue measure. The multi-dimensional analogue of E[∣A∣s]\mathbb{E}[|A|^s]E[∣A∣s] will now be

Renewal theory in less than one page

Let μ\muμ be a continuous probability distribution on R+\mathbb{R}_+R+​. Our goal is to show how renewal theory can be used to study convolution equations, represent their solutions with a probabilistic model, and draw consequences on their asymptotic behaviour. We are interested in finding fff such that

f(x)=g(x)+∫f(x−u)μ(du)f(x) = g(x) + \int f(x-u) \mu(du)f(x)=g(x)+∫f(x−u)μ(du)

where ggg is some function. Noting f⋆μ(x)=∫f(x−u)μ(du)f\star \mu(x) = \int f(x-u)\mu(du)f⋆μ(x)=∫f(x−u)μ(du), we recognize a convolution between the function fff and the measure μ\muμ, and the equation is simply f−μ⋆f=gf - \mu \star f = gf−μ⋆f=g. Upon noting UUU a random variable with distribution μ\muμ, we can also represent this equation as

f(x)=g(x)+E[f(x−U)]. f(x) = g(x) + \mathbb{E}[f(x-U)].f(x)=g(x)+E[f(x−U)].

But if this equation is satisfied for some fff, then plugging it into f(x−U)f(x-U)f(x−U) yields

f(x)=g(x)+E[g(x−U)+E[g(x−U−U′)]]=g(x)+E[g(x−U)]+E[g(x−U−U′)]+E[g(x−U−U′−U′′)]\begin{aligned}f(x) &= g(x) + \mathbb{E}[g(x-U) + \mathbb{E}[g(x - U - U')]] \\ &= g(x) + \mathbb{E}[g(x-U)] + \mathbb{E}[g(x - U - U')] + \mathbb{E}[g(x-U-U'-U'')] \end{aligned}f(x)​=g(x)+E[g(x−U)+E[g(x−U−U′)]]=g(x)+E[g(x−U)]+E[g(x−U−U′)]+E[g(x−U−U′−U′′)]​

The name "renewal process" comes from the fact that the SnS_nSn​ are considered as occurence times of events. The UiU_iUi​ are thus waiting times between the events. Renewal theory is interested in processes like Nt=N_t= Nt​= the number of events before time ttt.

It is quite rare that the representation theorem yields the explicit solution fff. One would need to compute the expectation of the series, which is most cases can be tedious. But this representation gives access to the whole toolbox of limit theorems in probability, and in particular, laws of large numbers, which translate into exact asymptotics for fff. That's the key renewal theorem.

  • « Directly Riemann integrable » is the true equivalent of « Riemann integrable », but for functions defined over R\mathbb{R}R or the half-line. It states that the Riemann sums over the whole domain (they are indeed series, not sums, in contrast with the classical definition) converge toward the same limit.

  • The intuition is pretty clear. In Blackwell's version, E[Nt+a−Nt]\mathbb{E}[N_{t+a} - N_t]E[Nt+a​−Nt​] is nothing but the number of SnS_nSn​ in the interval [t,t+a][t,t+a][t,t+a]. If the size of each jump is roughly E[U]\mathbb{E}[U]E[U] then the number of jumps in this interval should be proportional to the length of the interval divided by E[U]\mathbb{E}[U]E[U]; Blackwell's theorem says that this is true when t→∞t\to\inftyt→∞. Note that in this case g=1[−a,0]g=\mathbf{1}_[-a,0]g=1[​−a,0] and ∫g(u)du=a\int g(u)du = a∫g(u)du=a.

  • By linearity, Blackwell's version extends to (22) at least for piecewise constant functions with bounded support. To extend to wider functions, a limiting argument is needed, and this is where direct Riemann integrability comes into play.
