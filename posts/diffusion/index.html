Diffusion models

March 2023

These are my own notes for the formulation of the celebrated Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models; they're intended for mathematicians, hence the notations and style is quite different than in the original papers. In particular, I completely formulate everything as a continuous stochastic problem, then only in the end discretize the theoretical losses. This is exactly the opposite to what was done in the papers.

The problem

Let ppp be a probability density on Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd. The goal of generative modelling is twofold: given a training sample x1,…,xnx^1, \dotsc, x^nx1,…,xn of realizations of ppp, we want to

  1. learn ppp from the samples; 

  2. learn how to sample from ppp.

Let (pθ)(p_\theta)(pθ​) be a family of probability densities parametrized by θ\thetaθ; for example, one can choose pθp_\thetapθ​ to be the centered Gaussian distribution on Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd with covariance θ\thetaθ, although this family is not extremely expressive. One could also take a family of functions, say Eθ: Rd→RE_\theta : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}Eθ​: Rd→R, and set pθ(x)=e−Eθ(x)/Zθp_\theta(x) = e^{-E_\theta(x)}/Z_\thetapθ​(x)=e−Eθ​(x)/Zθ​ with Zθ=∫Rde−Eθ(y)dyZ_\theta = \int_{\mathbb{R}^d}e^{-E_\theta(y)}dyZθ​=∫Rd​e−Eθ​(y)dy. These are called energy-based models (EBMs). There are many ways in which we can parametrize a family of distribution functions, we'll go back at this problem later.

One the parametrization is chosen, our goal is to find the θ⋆\theta_\starθ⋆​ such that pθ⋆p_{\theta_\star}pθ⋆​​ is as close as possible to ppp. If we knew ppp, we would simply take

θ⋆∈arg min⁡θdist(p,pθ) \theta_\star \in \argmin_\theta \mathrm{dist}(p,p_\theta)θ⋆​∈θargmin​dist(p,pθ​)

where dist\mathrm{dist}dist is some may to measure the dissimilarity between distribution functions – it doesn't have to be a measure, it can be a divergence. Typically, one takes kl\mathrm{kl}kl, the Kullback-Leibler divergence: 

kl(p∣q)=∫p(x)log⁡(p(x)/q(x))dx. \mathrm{kl}(p\mid q) = \int p(x) \log (p(x)/q(x))dx.kl(p∣q)=∫p(x)log(p(x)/q(x))dx.

Minimizing kl(p∣pθ)\mathrm{kl}(p\mid p_\theta)kl(p∣pθ​) with respect to θ\thetaθ is the same thing as maximizing EX∼p[log⁡pθ(X)]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p}[\log p_\theta(X)]EX∼p​[logpθ​(X)] – both criteria yield the same optimal θ\thetaθ, called the maximum-likelihood estimator. Now, since we do not have access to ppp, we have to approximate it using p^=n−1∑δxi\hat{p} = n^{-1}\sum \delta_{x^i}p^​=n−1∑δxi​. The objective function becomes

ℓ(θ)=1n∑i=1nlog⁡pθ(xi).\ell(\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \log p_\theta(x^i). ℓ(θ)=n1​i=1∑n​logpθ​(xi).

Stochastic diffusions

SDEs transport the data distribution towards a near-Gaussian

Let (t,x)→ft(x)(t,x)\to f_t(x)(t,x)→ft​(x) and t→σtt\to \sigma_tt→σt​ be two smooth functions. Consider the stochastic differential equation

dXt=ft(Xt)dt+2σt2dBt,X0∼pdX_t = f_t(X_t)dt + \sqrt{2\sigma_t ^2}dB_t, \qquad \qquad X_0 \sim pdXt​=ft​(Xt​)dt+2σt2​​dBt​,X0​∼p

∂tpt(x)=Δ(σt2pt(x))−∇⋅(ft(x)pt(x)). \partial_t p_t(x) = \Delta (\sigma_t^2 p_t(x)) - \nabla \cdot (f_t(x)p_t(x)).∂t​pt​(x)=Δ(σt2​pt​(x))−∇⋅(ft​(x)pt​(x)).

Can you reverse this probability flow?

Given a sample XTX_TXT​ of pTp_TpT​ (which is well approximated by a Gaussian), we want to solve (6) backwards starting from XTX_TXT​, and we'll obtain a new sample X0X_0X0​ approximately distributed as p0p_0p0​. That would solve our second objective given in the introduction.

Now, there are many ways in which we can backward solve (6). First, we could note that (6) is nothing but the transport equation associated with a simple ODE.

Let x(t)x(t)x(t) be the solution of the differential equation with random initial condition x′(t)=vt(x(t))x(0)=X0.x'(t) = v_t(x(t))\qquad \qquad x(0) =X_0.x′(t)=vt​(x(t))x(0)=X0​. Then the probability density of x(t)x(t)x(t) is ptp_tpt​.
Proof. Let ptp_tpt​ be the probability density of xtx_txt​ and let φ\varphiφ be any smooth, compactly supported test function. Then, E[φ(x(t))]=∫pt(x)φ(x)dx\mathbb{E}[\varphi(x(t))] = \int p_t(x)\varphi(x)dxE[φ(x(t))]=∫pt​(x)φ(x)dx, so by derivation under the integral, ∫∂tpt(x)φ(x)dx=∂tE[φ(x(t))]=E[∇φ(x(t))x′(t)]=∫∇φ(x)vt(x)pt(x)dx=−∫φ(x)∇⋅(vt(x)pt(x))dx\begin{aligned}\int \partial_t p_t(x)\varphi(x)dx = \partial_t \mathbb{E}[\varphi(x(t))]&= \mathbb{E}[\nabla\varphi(x(t))x'(t)]\\ &= \int \nabla \varphi(x)v_t(x)p_t(x)dx = -\int \varphi(x) \nabla \cdot (v_t(x)p_t(x))dx \end{aligned}∫∂t​pt​(x)φ(x)dx=∂t​E[φ(x(t))]​=E[∇φ(x(t))x′(t)]=∫∇φ(x)vt​(x)pt​(x)dx=−∫φ(x)∇⋅(vt​(x)pt​(x))dx​ where the last line uses the multidimensional integration by parts formula (remember that ∇⋅\nabla \cdot∇⋅ is nothing but the divergence).

Simple ODEs can be reversed. The time-reversal of (12) is just y′(t)=−vt(y(t))y'(t) = -v_t(y(t))y′(t)=−vt​(y(t)). More precisely, the solution of the system

We could thus sample one realization of XTX_TXT​, or approximate it with a Gaussian, then solve the backward ODE, and obtain a near realization of X0X_0X0​. But solving (12) requires to know the flow vtv_tvt​, which we recall here when f(x)=−xf(x) = -xf(x)=−x:

vt(x)=σt2∇log⁡pt(x)+αtx. v_t(x) = \sigma_t^2 \nabla \log p_t(x) + \alpha_t x.vt​(x)=σt2​∇logpt​(x)+αt​x.

That looks like a miss: if we want to evaluate vtv_tvt​, we need to know ptp_tpt​ for all ttt, which requires knowledge of ppp. But thanks to the Gods of Statistics, we can very efficiently estimate ∇log⁡pt\nabla \log p_t∇logpt​. This is due to two things.

  1. First: we have samples from ptp_tpt​. Remember that our only information about ppp is a collection x1,…,xnx^1, \dotsc, x^nx1,…,xn of samples. But thanks to (9), we can represent xti=e−μtxi+σˉtξix^i_t = e^{-\mu_t}x^i + \bar{\sigma}_t \xi^ixti​=e−μt​xi+σˉt​ξi with ξi∼N(0,I)\xi^i \sim \mathscr{N}(0,I)ξi∼N(0,I) are samples from ptp_tpt​. They are extremely easy to access, since we only need to generate iid standard Gaussian variables ξi\xi^iξi.

  2. Second: score matching. If ppp is a probability density and xix^ixi are samples from ppp, estimating ∇log⁡p\nabla \log p∇logp (called score) has been thoroughly examined and is fairly doable, a technique known as score matching.

Learning the score

Vanilla score matching

Let ppp be a smooth probability density function supported over Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd and let XXX be a random variable with density ppp. The following elementary identity is due to Hyvärinen, 2005; it is the basis for score matching estimation in statistics.

Let s: Rd→Rds : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^ds: Rd→Rd be any smooth function with sufficiently fast decay at ∞\infty∞. Then, E[∣∇log⁡p(X)−s(X)∣2]=c+E[∣s(X)∣2+2div(s(X))] \mathbb{E}[\vert \nabla \log p(X) - s(X)\vert^2] = c + \mathbb{E}\left[|s(X)|^2 + 2 \mathrm{div}(s(X))\right] E[∣∇logp(X)−s(X)∣2]=c+E[∣s(X)∣2+2div(s(X))] where ccc is a constant not depending on sss.

Proof. We start by expanding the square norm: ∫p(x)∣∇log⁡p(x)−s(x)∣2dx=∫p(x)∣∇log⁡p(x)∣2dx+∫p(x)∣s(x)∣2dx−2∫∇log⁡p(x)⋅p(x)s(x)dx.\begin{aligned}\int p(x)|\nabla \log p(x) - s(x)|^2 dx &= \int p(x)|\nabla \log p(x)|^2 dx + \int p(x)|s(x)|^2 dx - 2\int \nabla \log p(x)\cdot p(x)s(x) dx. \end{aligned}∫p(x)∣∇logp(x)−s(x)∣2dx​=∫p(x)∣∇logp(x)∣2dx+∫p(x)∣s(x)∣2dx−2∫∇logp(x)⋅p(x)s(x)dx.​ The first term does not depend on sss, it is our constant ccc. For the last term, we use ∇log⁡p=∇p/p\nabla \log p = \nabla p / p∇logp=∇p/p then we use the integration-by-parts formula: 

2∫∇log⁡p(x)⋅p(x)s(x)dx=2∫∇p(x)⋅s(x)dx=−2∫p(x)(∇⋅s(x))dx2\int \nabla \log p(x)\cdot p(x)s(x) dx = 2\int \nabla p(x) \cdot s(x) dx = -2 \int p(x)( \nabla \cdot s(x))dx2∫∇logp(x)⋅p(x)s(x)dx=2∫∇p(x)⋅s(x)dx=−2∫p(x)(∇⋅s(x))dx

and the identity is proved.

Now, (16) is particularly interesting for us. Remember that if we want to reverse (12), we do not really need to estimate ptp_tpt​ but only ∇log⁡pt\nabla \log p_t∇logpt​. We do so by approximating it using a parametrized family of functions, say sθs_\thetasθ​ (typically, neural networks):

θt∈arg min⁡θE[∣∇log⁡pt(Xt)−sθ(Xt)∣2]=arg min⁡θE[∣sθ(Xt)∣2+2div(sθ(Xt))]. \theta_t \in \argmin_\theta \mathbb{E}[\vert \nabla \log p_t(X_t) - s_{\theta}(X_t)\vert^2] = \argmin_\theta \mathbb{E}[|s_{\theta}(X_t)|^2 + 2 \mathrm{div}(s_{\theta}(X_t))].θt​∈θargmin​E[∣∇logpt​(Xt​)−sθ​(Xt​)∣2]=θargmin​E[∣sθ​(Xt​)∣2+2div(sθ​(Xt​))].

Once we have θt\theta_tθt​, we can approximate the velocity field vtv_tvt​ with

v^t(x)=σt2sθt(x)+αtx,\hat{v}_t(x) = \sigma_t^2 s_{\theta_t}(x) + \alpha_t x,v^t​(x)=σt2​sθt​​(x)+αt​x,

then backward solve the approximated ODE

initialized at t=0t=0t=0 with Y0=N(0,σˉT2)Y_0 = \mathscr{N}(0,\bar{\sigma}_T^2)Y0​=N(0,σˉT2​), an approximation of XTX_TXT​.

How do you do empirically?

How do we solve (19)?

  1. First, we need not solve this optimization problem for every ttt. Denoising Score Matching

Fortunately, there is another way to perform score matching when ptp_tpt​ is the distribution of a random variable with gaussian noise added, as in our setting. We'll present this result in a fairly abstract setting; we suppose that ppp is a density function, and q=p∗gσq = p*g_\sigmaq=p∗gσ​ where gσg_\sigmagσ​ is the Gaussian density with variance σ\sigmaσ. The following result is due to Vincent, 2010.

Denoising Score Matching Objective

Let s:Rd→Rds:\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^ds:Rd→Rd be a smooth function. Let XXX be a random variable with density ppp, ε\varepsilonε an independent random variable with density ggg, and Xε=X+εX_\varepsilon = X + \varepsilonXε​=X+ε, whose density is pg=p∗gp_g = p * gpg​=p∗g. Proof. By the same computation as for vanilla score matching, we have

E[∣∇log⁡pg(Xε)−s(Xε)∣2]=c+∫pg(x)∣s(x)∣2dx−2∫∇pg(x)⋅s(x)dx. \mathbb{E}[\vert \nabla \log p_g(X_\varepsilon) - s(X_\varepsilon)\vert^2] = c + \int p_g(x)|s(x)|^2dx -2\int \nabla p_g(x)\cdot s(x)dx.E[∣∇logpg​(Xε​)−s(Xε​)∣2]=c+∫pg​(x)∣s(x)∣2dx−2∫∇pg​(x)⋅s(x)dx.

Now by definition, pg(x)=∫p(y)g(x−y)dyp_g(x) = \int p(y)g(x-y)dypg​(x)=∫p(y)g(x−y)dy, hence ∇pg(x)=∫p(y)∇g(x−y)dy\nabla p_g(x) = \int p(y)\nabla g(x-y)dy∇pg​(x)=∫p(y)∇g(x−y)dy, and the last term above is equal to −2∫∫p(y)∇g(x−y)⋅s(x)dxdy=−2∫∫p(y)g(x−y)∇log⁡g(x−y)⋅s(x)dydx=−2E[∇log⁡g(ε)⋅s(X+ε)].\begin{aligned} -2\int \int p(y)\nabla g(x-y)\cdot s(x)dxdy &= -2\int \int p(y)g(x-y)\nabla \log g(x-y)\cdot s(x)dydx\\ &= -2\mathbb{E}[\nabla \log g(\varepsilon)\cdot s(X + \varepsilon)]. \end{aligned}−2∫∫p(y)∇g(x−y)⋅s(x)dxdy​=−2∫∫p(y)g(x−y)∇logg(x−y)⋅s(x)dydx=−2E[∇logg(ε)⋅s(X+ε)].​ This last term is equal to −2E[∇log⁡g(ε)⋅s(Xε)]-2\mathbb{E}[\nabla \log g(\varepsilon)\cdot s(X_\varepsilon)]−2E[∇logg(ε)⋅s(Xε​)].
