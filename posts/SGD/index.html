Gradient descent II: stochastic gradient descent for convex functions

October 2023

We are now interested in the minimization of a function fff assuming a very specific shape, namely

f(x)=1n∑i=1mfi(x) f(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^m f_i(x)f(x)=n1​i=1∑m​fi​(x)

where the fi:Rd→Rf_i : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}fi​:Rd→R are convex functions. This situation arises in many machine learning algorithms where we wish to minimize an empirical loss.

If iii is a uniformly chosen index in {1,…,d}\{1, \dotsc, d\}{1,…,d}, then the random variable fi(x)f_i(x)fi​(x) is an unbiased estimator of f(x)f(x)f(x). This is the key to stochastic gradient descent algorithms: instead of going in the direction of the full gradient ∇f(x)\nabla f(x)∇f(x), which would require to evaluate the ddd gradients ∇fi(x)\nabla f_i(x)∇fi​(x) to get ∇f(x)\nabla f(x)∇f(x), we go in the direction of the partial gradient ∇fi(x)\nabla f_i(x)∇fi​(x). This kind of methods fall within the framework defined long ago by Robbins and Monro and there is a vast litterature on the topic. However, it turns out that convexity-type assumptions on the fif_ifi​ already yield reasonnable convergence results; the results are similar to the classical gradient descent result.

We recall that fff is η\etaη-strongly convex and MMM-smooth if ⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+η2∣x−y∣2⩽f(y)−f(x)⩽⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+M2∣x−y∣2. \langle \nabla f(x), y-x \rangle + \frac{\eta}{2}|x-y|^2 \leqslant f(y) - f(x) \leqslant \langle \nabla f(x), y-x \rangle + \frac{M}{2}|x-y|^2. ⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+2η​∣x−y∣2⩽f(y)−f(x)⩽⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+2M​∣x−y∣2.

In the sequel we will suppose that all the fif_ifi​ are η\etaη-strongly convex and MMM-smooth and it is easy to check that in this case, so is fff. We will denote by xxx the unique minimizer of fff. At this point, ∇f(x)=0\nabla f(x)=0∇f(x)=0.

For all nnn, we set in∼Unif{1,…,d}i_n \sim \mathrm{Unif}\{1,\dotsc, d\}in​∼Unif{1,…,d}. The SGD is given by the updates

xn+1=xn−εn∇fin(xn). x_{n+1} = x_n - \varepsilon_n \nabla f_{i_n}(x_n).xn+1​=xn​−εn​∇fin​​(xn​).

If all the fif_ifi​ are η\etaη-strongly convex and MMM-smooth, and if the step size ε\varepsilonε is smaller than 1/2M1/2M1/2M, then

E[∣xn−x∣2]⩽(1−εη)n∣x0−x∣2+2εησ \mathbb{E}[|x_n - x|^2] \leqslant (1 - \varepsilon \eta)^n |x_0 - x|^2 + \frac{2\varepsilon}{\eta}\sigmaE[∣xn​−x∣2]⩽(1−εη)n∣x0​−x∣2+η2ε​σ

where σ\sigmaσ is the local variance of fff around the minimum:

σ=Ej∼Unif{1,…,d}[∣∇fj(x)∣2].\sigma = \mathbb{E}_{j \sim \mathrm{Unif}\{1, \dotsc, d\}}[|\nabla f_j(x)|^2]. σ=Ej∼Unif{1,…,d}​[∣∇fj​(x)∣2].

What comes out of this theorem, compared with the non-stochastic gradient descent, is the 2εσ/η2\varepsilon\sigma / \eta2εσ/η term. Indeed, there can be no general convergence result for SGD since, even when xn=xx_n = xxn​=x, the dynamics does not stop because there is no reason for ∇fi(x)\nabla f_i(x)∇fi​(x) to be zero. However, the theorem says that eventually, xnx_nxn​ stays within distance roughly 2ε/η2\varepsilon / \eta2ε/η of the minimizer xxx.

The rest of the note is devoted to the proof of this result, which is marginally more technical than the classical exponential convergence of gradient descent. In the following note, I will prove yet another similar result, but where we considerably loosen the conditions on fff and in particular allow for non-convexity.

Proof

By developing the square norm ∣xn+1−x∣2=∣xn+1−xn+xn−x∣2|x_{n+1} - x|^2 = |x_{n+1} - x_n + x_n - x|^2∣xn+1​−x∣2=∣xn+1​−xn​+xn​−x∣2, we get

∣xn+1−x∣2=∣xn−x∣2−2ε⟨xn−x,∇fin(xn)⟩+ε2∣∇fin(xn)∣2.\begin{aligned} |x_{n+1} - x|^2 = |x_n - x|^2 - 2\varepsilon\langle x_n - x, \nabla f_{i_n}(x_n)\rangle + \varepsilon^2 | \nabla f_{i_n}(x_n)|^2. \end{aligned}∣xn+1​−x∣2=∣xn​−x∣2−2ε⟨xn​−x,∇fin​​(xn​)⟩+ε2∣∇fin​​(xn​)∣2.​

Let us take expectations conditionnally on xnx_nxn​; for simplicity we note En\mathbb{E}_nEn​ for the conditional expectation given all the information before timestep nnn. We have

En[∣xn+1−x∣2]=∣xn−x∣2−2ε⟨xn−x,En[∇fin(xn)]⟩+ε2En[∣∇fin(xn)∣2].\mathbb{E}_n[|x_{n+1} - x|^2 ]= |x_n - x|^2 - 2\varepsilon\langle x_n - x, \mathbb{E}_n[\nabla f_{i_n}(x_n)]\rangle + \varepsilon^2 \mathbb{E}_n[| \nabla f_{i_n}(x_n)|^2]. En​[∣xn+1​−x∣2]=∣xn​−x∣2−2ε⟨xn​−x,En​[∇fin​​(xn​)]⟩+ε2En​[∣∇fin​​(xn​)∣2].

Since ∇fin(x)\nabla f_{i_n}(x)∇fin​​(x) is an unbiased estimator of ∇fin(x)\nabla f_{i_n}(x)∇fin​​(x), the term in the middle is equal to −2ε⟨xn−x,∇f(xn)⟩-2\varepsilon\langle x_n - x, \nabla f(x_n)\rangle−2ε⟨xn​−x,∇f(xn​)⟩. By (2) applied to x=xnx=x_nx=xn​ and y=xy = xy=x (that's the same argument as for gradient descent).

−2ε⟨∇f(xn),xn−x⟩⩽2ε(f(x)−f(xn))−εη∣xn−x∣2 -2\varepsilon \langle \nabla f(x_n), x_n - x \rangle \leqslant 2\varepsilon(f(x) - f(x_n)) - \varepsilon\eta|x_n - x|^2−2ε⟨∇f(xn​),xn​−x⟩⩽2ε(f(x)−f(xn​))−εη∣xn​−x∣2

and we now have

En[∣xn+1−x∣2]⩽(1−εη)∣xn−x∣2+zn\mathbb{E}_n[|x_{n+1} - x|^2 ]\leqslant (1 - \varepsilon \eta) |x_n - x|^2 + z_n En​[∣xn+1​−x∣2]⩽(1−εη)∣xn​−x∣2+zn​

where

zn=−2ε(f(xn)−f(x))+ε2En[∣∇fin(xn)∣2]. z_n = - 2\varepsilon (f(x_n) - f(x)) + \varepsilon^2 \mathbb{E}_n[|\nabla f_{i_n}(x_n)|^2].zn​=−2ε(f(xn​)−f(x))+ε2En​[∣∇fin​​(xn​)∣2].

Bounding znz_nzn​ is not directly accessible due to the inherent randomness of the problem. However, we can bound E[zn]\mathbb{E}[z_n]E[zn​]. The key technical lemma will be the following:

For any jjj uniform over [d][d][d] and any yyy, E[∣∇fj(y)∣2]⩽4M(f(y)−f(x))+2σ. \mathbb{E}[|\nabla f_j(y)|^2 ] \leqslant 4 M (f(y) - f(x)) + 2\sigma . E[∣∇fj​(y)∣2]⩽4M(f(y)−f(x))+2σ.

Now we can finish the proof of the main theorem: indeed, directly plugging this estimate into the definition of E[zn]\mathbb{E}[z_n]E[zn​] yields

E[zn]⩽2ε2σ+E[f(xn)−f(x)]×(4ε2M−2ε)\mathbb{E}[z_n] \leqslant 2 \varepsilon^2 \sigma + \mathbb{E}[f(x_n) - f(x)]\times \left(4\varepsilon^2 M - 2\varepsilon \right) E[zn​]⩽2ε2σ+E[f(xn​)−f(x)]×(4ε2M−2ε)

and since we took ε<1/2M\varepsilon < 1/2Mε<1/2M, we finally get E[zn]⩽2ε2σ\mathbb{E}[z_n] \leqslant 2\varepsilon^2 \sigmaE[zn​]⩽2ε2σ and

E[∣xn+1−x∣2]⩽(1−εη)E[∣xn−x∣2]+2ε2σ. \mathbb{E}[|x_{n+1} - x|^2] \leqslant (1 - \varepsilon \eta)\mathbb{E}[|x_n - x|^2] + 2\varepsilon^2 \sigma.E[∣xn+1​−x∣2]⩽(1−εη)E[∣xn​−x∣2]+2ε2σ.

This is a classical recursion of the form un+1⩽aun+bu_{n+1} \leqslant a u_n + bun+1​⩽aun​+b. An easy recursion yields un⩽b(1−an)/(1−a)+anu0 u_n \leqslant b(1-a^n)/(1-a) + a^n u_0 un​⩽b(1−an)/(1−a)+anu0​, hence

Proof of the Lemma

Pick any yyy and random index jjj uniform over [d][d][d]. Then ∣∇fj(y)∣⩽∣∇fj(y)−∇fj(x)∣+∣∇fj(x)∣|\nabla f_j(y)| \leqslant |\nabla f_j (y) - \nabla f_j(x)| + |\nabla f_j(x)|∣∇fj​(y)∣⩽∣∇fj​(y)−∇fj​(x)∣+∣∇fj​(x)∣. Using (a+b)2⩽2a2+2b2(a+b)^2 \leqslant 2a^2 + 2b^2(a+b)2⩽2a2+2b2 then averaging we get

E∣∇fj(y)∣2⩽2E∣∇fj(y)−∇fj(x)∣2+2E∣∇fj(x)∣2.\mathbb{E}|\nabla f_j(y)|^2 \leqslant 2\mathbb{E}|\nabla f_j (y) - \nabla f_j(x)|^2 + 2\mathbb{E}|\nabla f_j(x)|^2.E∣∇fj​(y)∣2⩽2E∣∇fj​(y)−∇fj​(x)∣2+2E∣∇fj​(x)∣2.

Since E∇fi(x)=∇f(x)=0\mathbb{E}\nabla f_i(x) = \nabla f(x) = 0E∇fi​(x)=∇f(x)=0, the second term is 2σ⋆(f)2\sigma_\star(f)2σ⋆​(f), giving the second term in (11). Now, we have to bound the first term. This will come as a consequence of strong convexity and smoothness.

Lemma. Any convex MMM-smooth function ggg satisfies g(x)−g(y)⩽⟨∇g(x),x−y⟩−12M∣∇g(y)−∇g(x)∣2. g(x) - g(y) \leqslant \langle \nabla g(x), x-y\rangle - \frac{1}{2M}|\nabla g(y) - \nabla g(x)|^2. g(x)−g(y)⩽⟨∇g(x),x−y⟩−2M1​∣∇g(y)−∇g(x)∣2.

Proof. Indeed, let x,y,zx,y,zx,y,z be any points; introducing g(z)g(z)g(z) we have g(x)−g(y)=g(x)−g(z)+g(z)−g(y)g(x)-g(y) = g(x) - g(z) + g(z) - g(y)g(x)−g(y)=g(x)−g(z)+g(z)−g(y).

  • The first term is smaller than ⟨∇g(x),x−z⟩\langle \nabla g(x), x-z\rangle⟨∇g(x),x−z⟩ just by mere convexity.

  • By smoothness, g(z)−g(y)⩽⟨∇g(y),z−y⟩+M∣z−y∣2/2g(z) - g(y) \leqslant \langle \nabla g(y), z-y\rangle + M|z-y|^2/2g(z)−g(y)⩽⟨∇g(y),z−y⟩+M∣z−y∣2/2.

We thus have

g(x)−g(y)⩽⟨∇g(x),x−z⟩+⟨∇g(y),z−y⟩+M∣z−y∣2/2g(x) - g(y) \leqslant \langle \nabla g(x), x-z\rangle + \langle \nabla g(y), z-y\rangle + M|z-y|^2/2g(x)−g(y)⩽⟨∇g(x),x−z⟩+⟨∇g(y),z−y⟩+M∣z−y∣2/2

and this upper bound is quadratic in zzz so it's readily minimized; the minimum is attained at the point

z=y−∇g(y)−∇g(x)Mz = y - \frac{\nabla g(y) - \nabla g(x)}{M}z=y−M∇g(y)−∇g(x)​

and the corresponding value of the upper bound is (16).

Apply the lemma to g=fjg = f_jg=fj​: 

∣∇fj(y)−∇fj(x)∣2⩽2M(fj(y)−fj(x))+2M⟨∇fj(x),x−y⟩. |\nabla f_j(y) - \nabla f_j(x)|^2 \leqslant 2M(f_j(y) - f_j(x)) + 2M \langle \nabla f_j(x), x-y\rangle.∣∇fj​(y)−∇fj​(x)∣2⩽2M(fj​(y)−fj​(x))+2M⟨∇fj​(x),x−y⟩.

We now average over the random index jjj; the average of the upper bound is

2M(f(y)−f(x))+2M⟨∇f(x),x−y⟩=2M(f(y)−f(x)) 2M (f(y) - f(x)) + 2M \langle \nabla f(x), x-y\rangle = 2M(f(y) - f(x))2M(f(y)−f(x))+2M⟨∇f(x),x−y⟩=2M(f(y)−f(x))

since ∇f(x)=0\nabla f(x) = 0∇f(x)=0. Plugging this in (15) gives (11).
