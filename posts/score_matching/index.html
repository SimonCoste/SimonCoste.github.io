Flow models II: Score Matching Techniques

March 2025

Let ppp be any smooth probability density function. Its score is the gradient of its log-density: ∇log⁡p(x)\nabla \log p(x)∇logp(x). This object is of paramount importance in many fields, like physics, statistics, and machine learning. In particular, it is the key to sample from ppp using the Langevin dynamics, and we've seen it to be the key when reversing diffusion processes. In this note, we survey the classical technique used for learning the score of a density from its samples: score matching.

The Fisher divergence

The L2-distance between the scores of two probability densities is often called the Fisher divergence: 

fisher(ρ1∣ρ2)=∫ρ1(x)∣∇log⁡ρ1(x)−∇log⁡ρ2(x)∣2dx. \mathrm{fisher}(\rho_1 \mid \rho_2) = \int \rho_1(x)|\nabla\log\rho_1(x) - \nabla\log\rho_2(x)|^2dx.fisher(ρ1​∣ρ2​)=∫ρ1​(x)∣∇logρ1​(x)−∇logρ2​(x)∣2dx.

Since our goal is to learn ∇log⁡p(x)\nabla\log p(x)∇logp(x), it is natural to choose a parametrized family of functions sθs_\thetasθ​ and to optimize θ\thetaθ so that the divergence

∫p(x)∣∇log⁡p(x)−sθ(x)∣2dx\int p(x)|\nabla\log p(x) - s_\theta(x)|^2dx ∫p(x)∣∇logp(x)−sθ​(x)∣2dx

is as small as possible. However, this optimization problem is intractable, due to the presence of the explicit form of ppp inside the integral. This is where Score Matching techniques come into play.

Vanilla score matching

Let ppp be a smooth probability density function supported over Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd and let XXX be a random variable with density ppp. The following elementary identity is due to Hyvärinen, 2005; it is the basis for score matching estimation in statistics.

Let s: Rd→Rds : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^ds: Rd→Rd be any smooth function with sufficiently fast decay at ∞\infty∞, and X∼pX \sim pX∼p. Then, E[∣∇log⁡p(X)−s(X)∣2]=c+E[∣s(X)∣2+2∇⋅s(X)] \mathbb{E}[\vert \nabla \log p(X) - s(X)\vert^2] = c + \mathbb{E}\left[|s(X)|^2 + 2 \nabla \cdot s(X)\right] E[∣∇logp(X)−s(X)∣2]=c+E[∣s(X)∣2+2∇⋅s(X)] where ccc is a constant not depending on sss.

Proof. We start by expanding the square norm: ∫p(x)∣∇log⁡p(x)−s(x)∣2dx=∫p(x)∣∇log⁡p(x)∣2dx+∫p(x)∣s(x)∣2dx−2∫∇log⁡p(x)⋅p(x)s(x)dx.\begin{aligned}\int p(x)|\nabla \log p(x) - s(x)|^2 dx &= \int p(x)|\nabla \log p(x)|^2 dx + \int p(x)|s(x)|^2 dx - 2\int \nabla \log p(x)\cdot p(x)s(x) dx. \end{aligned}∫p(x)∣∇logp(x)−s(x)∣2dx​=∫p(x)∣∇logp(x)∣2dx+∫p(x)∣s(x)∣2dx−2∫∇logp(x)⋅p(x)s(x)dx.​ The first term does not depend on sss, it is our constant ccc. For the last term, we use ∇log⁡p=∇p/p\nabla \log p = \nabla p / p∇logp=∇p/p then we use the integration-by-parts formula: 

2∫∇log⁡p(x)⋅p(x)s(x)dx=2∫∇p(x)⋅s(x)dx=−2∫p(x)(∇⋅s(x))dx2\int \nabla \log p(x)\cdot p(x)s(x) dx = 2\int \nabla p(x) \cdot s(x) dx = -2 \int p(x)( \nabla \cdot s(x))dx2∫∇logp(x)⋅p(x)s(x)dx=2∫∇p(x)⋅s(x)dx=−2∫p(x)(∇⋅s(x))dx

and the identity is proved.

The loss we want to minimize is thus

θ∈arg min⁡θE[∣∇log⁡p(X)−sθ(X)∣2]=arg min⁡θE[∣sθ(X)∣2+2∇⋅(sθ(X))]. \theta \in \argmin_\theta \mathbb{E}[\vert \nabla \log p(X) - s_{\theta}(X)\vert^2] = \argmin_\theta \mathbb{E}[|s_{\theta}(X)|^2 + 2 \nabla \cdot (s_{\theta}(X))].θ∈θargmin​E[∣∇logp(X)−sθ​(X)∣2]=θargmin​E[∣sθ​(X)∣2+2∇⋅(sθ​(X))].

Practically, learning the score of a density ppp from samples x1,…,xnx^1, \dots, x^nx1,…,xn is done by minimizing the empirical version of the right-hand side of (3):

ℓ(θ)=1n∑i=1n∣sθ(xi)∣2+2∇⋅(sθ(xi)). \ell(\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n |s_{\theta}(x^i)|^2 + 2 \nabla \cdot (s_{\theta}(x^i)).ℓ(θ)=n1​i=1∑n​∣sθ​(xi)∣2+2∇⋅(sθ​(xi)).

In the context of diffusion models, we have a whole family of densities ptp_tpt​ and we want to learn the score for every t∈[0,T]t \in [0,T]t∈[0,T].

  1. First, we need not solve this optimization problem for every ttt. which looks computable… except it's not ideal. Suppose we perform a gradient descent on θ\thetaθ to find the optimal θ\thetaθ for time ttt. In high dimension, this can be too costly.

Noisy samples: denoising score matching and Tweedie's formula

Denoising Score Matching

Fortunately, there is another way to perform score matching when ptp_tpt​ is the distribution of a random variable with gaussian noise added, as in our setting. We'll present this result in a fairly abstract setting; we suppose that ppp is a density function, and q=p∗gq = p*gq=p∗g where ggg is an other density. The following result is due to Vincent, 2010.

Denoising Score Matching Objective

Let s:Rd→Rds:\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^ds:Rd→Rd be a smooth function. Let XXX be a random variable with density ppp, and let ε\varepsilonε be an independent random variable with density ggg. We call pnoisyp_{\mathrm{noisy}}pnoisy​ the distribution of Xnoisy=X+εX_{\mathrm{noisy}}=X + \varepsilonXnoisy​=X+ε. Then, E[∣∇log⁡pnoisy(X+ε)−s(X+ε)∣2]=c+E[∣∇log⁡g(ε)−s(X+ε)∣2] \mathbb{E}[\vert \nabla \log p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(X+\varepsilon) - s(X+\varepsilon)\vert^2] = c + \mathbb{E}[|\nabla \log g(\varepsilon) - s(X+\varepsilon)|^2] E[∣∇logpnoisy​(X+ε)−s(X+ε)∣2]=c+E[∣∇logg(ε)−s(X+ε)∣2] where ccc is a constant not depending on sss.

Proof. Note that pnoisy=p∗gp_{\mathrm{noisy}} = p * gpnoisy​=p∗g. By expanding the square, we see that E[∣∇log⁡pnoisy(X+ε)−s(X+ε)∣2] \mathbb{E}[\vert \nabla \log p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(X+\varepsilon) - s(X+\varepsilon)\vert^2]E[∣∇logpnoisy​(X+ε)−s(X+ε)∣2] is equal to

c+∫pnoisy(x)∣s(x)∣2dx−2∫∇pnoisy(x)⋅s(x)dx. c + \int p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(x)|s(x)|^2dx -2\int \nabla p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(x)\cdot s(x)dx.c+∫pnoisy​(x)∣s(x)∣2dx−2∫∇pnoisy​(x)⋅s(x)dx.

Now by definition, pnoisy(x)=∫p(y)g(x−y)dyp_{\mathrm{noisy}}(x) = \int p(y)g(x-y)dypnoisy​(x)=∫p(y)g(x−y)dy, hence ∇pnoisy(x)=∫p(y)∇g(x−y)dy\nabla p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(x) = \int p(y)\nabla g(x-y)dy∇pnoisy​(x)=∫p(y)∇g(x−y)dy, and the last term above is equal to −2∫∫p(y)∇g(x−y)⋅s(x)dxdy=−2∫∫p(y)g(x−y)∇log⁡g(x−y)⋅s(x)dydx=−2E[∇log⁡g(ε)⋅s(X+ε)].\begin{aligned} -2\int \int p(y)\nabla g(x-y)\cdot s(x)dxdy &= -2\int \int p(y)g(x-y)\nabla \log g(x-y)\cdot s(x)dydx\\ &= -2\mathbb{E}[\nabla \log g(\varepsilon)\cdot s(X + \varepsilon)]. \end{aligned}−2∫∫p(y)∇g(x−y)⋅s(x)dxdy​=−2∫∫p(y)g(x−y)∇logg(x−y)⋅s(x)dydx=−2E[∇logg(ε)⋅s(X+ε)].​ But then, upon adding and subtracting the term E[∣∇log⁡g(ε)∣2]\mathbb{E}[|\nabla \log g(\varepsilon)|^2]E[∣∇logg(ε)∣2] which does not depend on sss, we get another constant c′c'c′ such that

E[∣∇log⁡pnoisy(X)−s(X)∣2]=c′+E[∣∇log⁡g(ε)−s(X+ε)∣2]. \mathbb{E}[\vert \nabla \log p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(X) - s(X)\vert^2] = c' + \mathbb{E}[|\nabla \log g(\varepsilon) - s(X + \varepsilon)|^2].E[∣∇logpnoisy​(X)−s(X)∣2]=c′+E[∣∇logg(ε)−s(X+ε)∣2].

Tweedie's formula

It turns out that the Denoising Score Matching objective is just an avatar of a deep, not so-well-known result, called Tweedie's formula. Herbert Robbins is often credited with the first discovery of this formula in the context of exponential (Poisson) distributions; Maurice Tweedie extended it, and Bradley Efron popularized it in his excellent paper on selection bias.

Tweedie's formula

Let XXX be a random variable with density ppp, and let ε\varepsilonε be an independent random variable with distribution N(0,σ2)N(0, \sigma^2)N(0,σ2). We still note pnoisyp_{\mathrm{noisy}}pnoisy​ the distribution of Xnoisy=X+εX_{\mathrm{noisy}} = X + \varepsilonXnoisy​=X+ε. Then, E[X∣Xnoisy]=Xnoisy+σ2∇ln⁡pnoisy(Xnoisy). \mathbb{E}[X \mid X_{\mathrm{noisy}}] = X_{\mathrm{noisy}} + \sigma^2 \nabla \ln p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(X_{\mathrm{noisy}}). E[X∣Xnoisy​]=Xnoisy​+σ2∇lnpnoisy​(Xnoisy​). Equivalently, E[ε∣Xnoisy]=−σ2∇ln⁡pnoisy(Xnoisy). \mathbb{E}\left[\left. \varepsilon \right| X_{\mathrm{noisy}}\right] = -\sigma^2 \nabla \ln p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(X_{\mathrm{noisy}}). E[ε∣Xnoisy​]=−σ2∇lnpnoisy​(Xnoisy​).

Proof. The joint distribution of (X,Xnoisy)(X, X_{\mathrm{noisy}})(X,Xnoisy​) is p(x)g(z−x)p(x)g(z-x)p(x)g(z−x), hence the conditional expectation of XXX given Xnoisy=zX_{\mathrm{noisy}} = zXnoisy​=z is

∫xp(x)g(z−x)dxq(z)=z−∫(z−x)g(z−x)p(x)dxpnoisy(z).\frac{\int x p(x)g(z-x)dx}{q(z)} = z - \frac{\int (z-x)g(z-x)p(x)dx}{p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(z)}.q(z)∫xp(x)g(z−x)dx​=z−pnoisy​(z)∫(z−x)g(z−x)p(x)dx​.

Note that pnoisy(z)=∫p(x)g(z−x)dxp_{\mathrm{noisy}}(z) = \int p(x)g(z-x)dxpnoisy​(z)=∫p(x)g(z−x)dx. Now, since ggg is the density of N(0,σ2Id)N(0,\sigma^2 I_d)N(0,σ2Id​), we have ∇g(x)=−x/σ2g(x)\nabla g(x) = -x/\sigma^2 g(x)∇g(x)=−x/σ2g(x), and the term above is equal to

z+σ2∫∇zg(z−x)p(x)dxpnoisy(z)=z+σ2∇zln⁡∫g(z−x)p(x)dxz +\sigma^2 \frac{\int \nabla_z g(z-x)p(x)dx}{p_{\mathrm{noisy}}(z)} = z + \sigma^2 \nabla_z \ln \int g(z-x)p(x)dxz+σ2pnoisy​(z)∫∇z​g(z−x)p(x)dx​=z+σ2∇z​ln∫g(z−x)p(x)dx

which is the desired result. For the second formula, just note that Xnoisy=E[Xnoisy∣Xnoisy]=E[X∣Xnoisy]+E[ε∣Xnoisy]X_{\mathrm{noisy}} = \mathbb{E}[X_{\mathrm{noisy}}|X_{\mathrm{noisy}}] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid X_{\mathrm{noisy}}] + \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon \mid X_{\mathrm{noisy}}]Xnoisy​=E[Xnoisy​∣Xnoisy​]=E[X∣Xnoisy​]+E[ε∣Xnoisy​] then simplify.

Since XnoisyX_{\mathrm{noisy}}Xnoisy​ is centered around XXX, the classical ("frequentist") estimate for XXX given XnoisyX_{\mathrm{noisy}}Xnoisy​ is XnoisyX_{\mathrm{noisy}}Xnoisy​. Tweedie's formula corrects this estimate by adding a term accounting for the fact that XXX is itself random: for instance, if XnoisyX_{\mathrm{noisy}}Xnoisy​ lands in a region where XXX is extremely unlikely to live in, then it is probable that the noise is responsible for this, and the estimate for XXX.

Now, what's the link between this and Denoising Score Matching ? Well, the conditional expectation of any XXX given ZZZ minimizes the L2L^2L2-distance, in the sense that E[X∣Z]=f(Z)\mathbb{E}[X \mid Z] = f(Z)E[X∣Z]=f(Z) where fff minimizes E[∣f(Z)−X∣2]\mathbb{E}[|f(Z) - X|^2]E[∣f(Z)−X∣2]. Formula (15) says that ∇ln⁡pnoisy\nabla \ln p_{\mathrm{noisy}}∇lnpnoisy​, the quantity we need to estimate, is nothing but the « best denoiser » up to a scaling −σ2-\sigma^2−σ2: 

∇ln⁡pnoisy∈arg⁡min⁡E[∣f(X+ε)−(−ε/σ2)∣2]. \nabla \ln p_{\mathrm{noisy}} \in \arg \min \mathbb{E}[|f(X + \varepsilon) - (-\varepsilon/\sigma^2)|^2].∇lnpnoisy​∈argminE[∣f(X+ε)−(−ε/σ2)∣2].

Replacing fff with a neural network, we exactly find the Denoising Score Matching objective in (10).

I find this result to give some intuition on score matching and how to parametrize the network.

  • If s(x)s(x)s(x) is « noise predictor » trained with the objective E[∣s(X+ε)−ε∣2]\mathbb{E}[|s(X+\varepsilon) - \varepsilon|^2]E[∣s(X+ε)−ε∣2], then −s(x)/σ2-s(x)/\sigma^2−s(x)/σ2 is a proxy for ∇ln⁡pnoisy\nabla \ln p_{\mathrm{noisy}}∇lnpnoisy​. This is called « noise prediction » training.

  • If s(x)s(x)s(x) had rather been trained on a pure denoising objective E[∣s(X+ε)−X∣2]\mathbb{E}[|s(X+\varepsilon) - X|^2]E[∣s(X+ε)−X∣2], then x−s(x)x - s(x)x−s(x) is a noise predictor for XXX, hence σ−2(s(x)−x)\sigma^{-2} (s(x) - x)σ−2(s(x)−x) is a proxy for ∇ln⁡pnoisy\nabla \ln p_{\mathrm{noisy}}∇lnpnoisy​. This is called « data prediction » training, or simply denoising training.

Both formulations are found in the litterature, as well as affine mixes of both.
